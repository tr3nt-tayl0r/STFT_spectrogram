Imagine you’re listening to a song.

At any moment, you can hear many notes and sounds at once, but they change over time.
Some notes start, others stop.
You might hear a guitar, then a drum, then a voice.

The Fourier Transform is a math tool that can tell you what frequencies (pitches) are in a sound, along with their magnitudes.
But there’s a problem: it doesn’t tell you when those sounds happened...

That’s where the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) comes in.
Think of it like this: You slide a little window along the audio, like looking at one short moment at a time. In each little piece, you use the Fourier Transform to figure out the frequencies. Then you move the window a bit and repeat.
It’s like taking snapshots of the sound over time, each showing what pitches were present in that moment.

The result? You get a spectrogram – a cool picture that shows:
  • Time (left to right)
  • Pitch (up and down)
  • Loudness (color or brightness)

It’s like turning sound into a painting that shows how music or speech changes over time.
